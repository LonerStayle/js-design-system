The Quiet Vice of the Marginalia

On the sacred heresy of writing in books — and what the annotations of the dead reveal about the living who made them.

I. On the Nature of Marginalia

There is a particular quality of silence that settles over a reader who has just discovered, in the margin of a borrowed library book, evidence of a prior reader's passage — a pencilled exclamation, a corrective underline, a small and furious question mark beside a line of argument that had seemed, to you, perfectly reasonable. It is the silence of confrontation, of finding oneself suddenly in conversation with a ghost. The margin, that slender white corridor running beside the printed text, has always been the domain of the intimate and the unguarded. It is where thinkers think aloud, where scholars quarrel with the dead, where readers confess what they would never say in a lecture hall.

The word itself carries its etymology openly: margo, the Latin for edge or border. But there is nothing marginal about what is written there. In the great tradition of Western scholarship, the margin is often where the most vital conversation takes place — where Erasmus argued with Jerome, where Newton wrestled with Descartes, where Keats wrote the poems he could not yet write. The printed text may be the occasion, but the marginalia is the event.[1]

To write in a book is, of course, to commit a small sacrilege — or so the librarians have always maintained. The volumes behind their glass cases, the manuscripts in their temperature-controlled vaults, are to be approached with clean hands and a humbled eye. We are custodians, they remind us. The books do not belong to us. And yet the most treasured objects in any rare books library are precisely those volumes that have been most extensively violated by the attentions of their previous owners. The marginalia of past readers are what give an old book its soul.

II. The Annotators of History

Consider the case of Gabriel Harvey (MDXLV–MDCXXX), the Elizabethan scholar whose annotated copies of Livy, Guicciardini, and Machiavelli survive in the British Library.[2] Harvey was, by all contemporary accounts, an insufferable man — pedantic, vain, and prone to inserting himself into literary quarrels that might have been better left alone. Edmund Spenser tolerated him. Thomas Nashe loathed him. But Harvey's marginalia are among the most extraordinary documents of English Renaissance thought, because in them we see a mind of formidable energy working directly upon the texts that shaped his age.

He wrote in multiple colours of ink, in several languages, returning to the same passages across decades. A note written in his youth becomes, in his middle age, the object of his own later correction, annotated anew. The margin becomes a palimpsest, a record of a mind in argument with itself across time. Reading Harvey's books is less like reading scholarship than like eavesdropping on a very long and occasionally heated internal monologue.

A booke without annotations is like a countrie without inhabitants: rich in soile, but barren of culture — the land lies fallow, waiting for a mind to till it.

Gabriel Harvey, written in the flyleaf of his copy of Livy's Ab Urbe Condita, c. MDLXX

Harvey's practice was not unusual for his time. The Renaissance scholar's relationship to the printed book was participatory in a way that modern readers, trained to respect the integrity of the text, find difficult to imagine. The studia humanitatis demanded active engagement; the book was an interlocutor, not an oracle. To read without annotating was, in some learned circles, considered a form of intellectual laziness, a passive receipt of knowledge rather than its active digestion and transformation.

We find this practice across cultures and centuries. The Islamic tradition of ta'liq — the commentary appended to a text — produced entire libraries of marginalia that were eventually canonised into independent scholarly works. The Talmud itself is, in structural terms, an institutionalised marginal commentary on the Torah, the notes having grown to the point where they enfolded and finally displaced the original in cultural significance. Even the Homeric texts that survived antiquity came to us embedded in their scholiasts — the learned readers of Alexandria and Byzantium whose notes crowded the margins and eventually became inseparable from the works they annotated.

III. The Architecture of Forgotten Libraries

It is a commonplace of library history that the great collections of the ancient world were destroyed: Alexandria burned, Pergamon dispersed, the libraries of Carthage salted with the city that contained them. We mourn these losses in the abstract, the way one mourns a relative one never knew — with genuine feeling but without specific grief. But the truth of what was lost is more subtle, and more terrible, than the simple subtraction of texts.[3]

What was lost was not merely the books but the reading of the books — the accumulated understanding built across generations of scholars working on the same texts, the marginalia that recorded disputes now irrecoverable, the annotations that once guided interpretation and now are simply gone. Every destroyed library takes with it not only the works it housed but the entire conversational edifice erected around them.

† The Library of Antioch, destroyed by the Emperor Jovian in CCCLXIII, is said to have contained the complete works of Aristotle in the original — including the lost second book of the Poetics on Comedy.

The medieval monasteries understood something of this. The scriptoria of Cluny and Monte Cassino were not merely copying houses; they were engines of perpetuation. The monks who spent their days reproducing texts also spent their hours reading them, and the copies they produced were invariably annotated — the glosses interlinear, the commentary marginal, the correction continuous. The physical act of writing a text produced understanding of it that mere reading did not, and that understanding left its traces in the manuscript itself.

Umberto Eco, who knew his libraries, understood that a collection of books is always also a collection of absences. The gaps on a scholar's shelves tell as much as the volumes that fill them. A library reveals its owner's intellectual anxieties, the books deliberately not acquired, the subjects avoided, the rivals unread. And the marginalia within those volumes make this portrait still more intimate: here is not merely what was read but what was thought about what was read — the resistances, the agreements, the sudden illuminations that sent the reader back to a shelf already visited.

The library is not a place where books are stored. It is a place where books are remembered — by the institution, yes, but also and more vitally by the readers who return to them, generation upon generation, each leaving some ghost of their passage in the margins.

Dr. Miriam Solloway, The Memory Palace: Libraries and the Architecture of Knowledge (Oxford University Press, MCMXCVI), p. 274

The great private libraries of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — those assembled by scholars like John Dee, Robert Cotton, and Richard Heber — were understood by their owners not as collections but as extended minds. Cotton's library, which formed the nucleus of what would eventually become the British Library, was so intimately connected with its owner's intellectual life that its arrangement, by the busts of Roman emperors above each press, seemed less like a cataloguing system than an autobiography.

IV. A Catalogue of Annotations

In the interest of scholarly precision, I offer here a brief formal catalogue of the major annotation types encountered in the European manuscript tradition, following the taxonomy proposed by Professors Dagenais and Sherman in their respective studies of the annotated codex.[4]

# TYPOLOGIA ANNOTATIONUM — Margrave Classification System, rev. MCMXCVIII TYPE Nota Bene :: Attention marker; simple vertical stroke or 'NB' TYPE Manicule :: Pointing hand (☞); directs eye to key passage TYPE Interlinearis :: Gloss above or below the text line; typically lexical TYPE Marginalis :: Extended commentary in the margin; analytical TYPE Correctio :: Scribal or reader correction of perceived error TYPE Crux Interpretum :: Cross-mark (†) indicating crux; disputed passage TYPE Exclamatio :: Single or multiple exclamation; emotional response TYPE Quaestio :: Question mark; scepticism or genuine inquiry TYPE Cross-reference :: Locus (place) citation; binds text to tradition TYPE Florilegia :: Passage extracted for commonplace book; double bracket # Period distribution: Antiquity (I–VI c.) · Medieval (VII–XIV c.) # Humanist (XV–XVI c.) · Modern (XVII–present)

Each type carries its own phenomenology of reading. The manicule — that strange little hand, index finger extended, that appears in manuscripts from the ninth century through the eighteenth — is perhaps the most poignant of all annotation types. It is pure direction: look here. Nothing more is said. The hand points at a passage the annotator considered important, but offers no explanation of why, no gloss, no commentary. We are left to deduce importance from gesture alone.

The crux interpretum, marked by a dagger symbol, is its philosophical opposite. Where the manicule says "look here," the crux says "I do not know what to make of this." It is the annotation of intellectual honesty, the scholar's admission that the text has defeated them at this point, that the passage is contested or obscure or simply beyond their current understanding. To mark a crux is to leave a problem for the future — both one's own future self and whoever might read after.

† The Latin term crux here derives from the shape of the mark (†) rather than from any penitential connotation, though the pun was surely not lost on medieval monks.

V. The Beautiful Heresy

I confess: I write in books. Not borrowed books, not library books — I am not a monster — but my own books, the ones that line the study where I write these words. I write in pencil, not pen, partly out of residual guilt and partly because pencil permits a certain tentativeness that ink does not.[5] A pencilled note can be erased; a thought in ink is permanent, and permanence demands a confidence I rarely feel at the moment of reading.

My annotations are, I suspect, modest compared to Harvey's. I mark passages I want to return to. I note connections to other books. I occasionally argue in the margin with an argument that seems to me insufficiently supported, and I sometimes draw a small star beside sentences that achieve what I think of as the ideal of prose — the moment when the right words arrive in the right order and a complex truth is expressed with perfect economy. I have no system. The taxonomy I offered in the preceding section is descriptive, not autobiographical.

But the act of annotation is, I find, inseparable from the act of understanding. To mark a passage is to commit to it, to say: this matters to me, in this way, at this moment. The very labour of setting pencil to paper slows the reading eye and forces a kind of deliberateness that passive reading does not require. Annotation is, in this sense, a physical practice of attention — a discipline of the body as much as the mind.

There is a countervailing view, advanced most eloquently by Alberto Manguel in A History of Reading, that the annotated copy is a kind of palimpsest, the later reader's thoughts threatening to overlay and distort the earlier text. When we find a copy of Keats heavily annotated by a Victorian scholar who found the poet insufficiently moral, we read both Keats and the Victorian at once, and the risk is that the annotator's voice — shrill, persistent, inhabiting every margin — might drown the original. The margins, on this view, are contested territory.

Manguel is not wrong. But I think the risk is overstated, and in any case, the alternative — the pristine, unannotated volume that bears no trace of reading — is a kind of death. A book that has not been read is a building that has not been inhabited: structurally sound, perhaps, but without the quality of life that habitation brings. The slightly battered spine, the pencilled star, the small argumentative question mark — these are the signs of a book's career in the world, its passage through mind after mind.

VI. In Conclusion

I have been arguing, I realise, for something that requires no argument among those who already practise it, and that no argument will convince those who do not. The marginalist and the purist look at the same annotated page and see entirely different things: the one sees evidence of engagement, a record of thought, a palimpsest of reading across time; the other sees desecration, the text defaced, the reading experience of subsequent users contaminated by the intrusions of a prior ego.

Perhaps the truth is that both are right, and the discomfort of the annotated book is precisely its value. It refuses to be merely an object. It insists on being a transaction, a record of exchange between mind and text, text and time. When we discover the annotations of a previous reader, we are not simply reading about the past; we are encountering it in its most intimate register — the private, provisional, unguarded thought of someone who sat where we now sit, with roughly the same candle burning and the same darkness outside the window, and found this sentence worth noting.

There is something almost sacramental in this. The annotated book is a kind of secular relic — it has been touched, attended to, marked by a specific intelligence at a specific moment, and in handling it we enter into contact with that specificity. Libraries guard these objects not merely because they are old or rare or beautiful, though they are often all three, but because they are irreplaceable records of the activity of reading itself.

In the end, the marginalia is what the book becomes when it meets a mind. The text is the potential; the annotation is the actualisation. And it is in the margin — that slender white corridor beside the word — that the life of literature is most fully and most honestly lived.[6]

Dr. Eleanor Margrave holds the Cavendish Chair of Antiquarian Studies at Merton College, Oxford. She is the author of Readers and Their Books: An Anatomy of Annotation (Clarendon Press, MCMXCIV) and The Scholarship of the Margin (Oxford University Press, MMIII). She is currently completing a study of Scottish Enlightenment reading practices, tentatively entitled What the Dead Have Underlined. Her personal library, housed in a converted Victorian coach house in North Oxford, contains some fourteen thousand volumes, most of them annotated.
