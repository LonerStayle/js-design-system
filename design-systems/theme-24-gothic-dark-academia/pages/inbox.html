Re: The Aldine Virgil — Authentication & Provenance
Professor Edmund Ashworth · e.ashworth@magdalen.ox.ac.uk
To: Dr. R. Blackthorn  ·  14th November, Anno Domini MDCCCXCII  ·  Magdalen College, Oxford

I have examined the colophon most carefully, as you requested, and I confess the doubts which you raised in your previous communication have only deepened upon closer scrutiny. The Aldine device — that handsome anchor and dolphin, festina lente, which old Aldus set upon his title pages with such proud economy — presents certain irregularities in the present specimen that give me considerable pause. The ink itself is consistent with a late fifteenth-century Venetian printing house, yet the quiring speaks against it. Allow me to elaborate at some length, for this matter touches upon the very authenticity of what may be the finest acquisition your institution has made in living memory, or alternatively, its most costly embarrassment.

The watermark in gathering C — a crowned eagle rampant, bearing a shield charged with a cross of St. George — is, I am reliably informed, peculiar to the Treviso paper-mills of Bartolomeo Moranzone, whose output was extensively documented by the late Professor Kuchenbecker in his monumental Papiergeschichte des Veneto of 1871. This watermark, crucially, does not appear in any documented Aldine imprint before the year 1501, whereas the colophon before us claims a date of MCCCCXCVIII. The discrepancy is not merely a curiosity; it is, if I am not greatly mistaken, a damnation. Forgeries of the Aldine press were, I need not remind you, a considerable industry in the seventeenth century, particularly in Lyon, where a certain master printer whose name historians continue to dispute produced some fifty simulacra of considerable virtuosity.

And yet — and here I must urge you to resist the temptation of premature despair — there remains one possibility which the forger hypothesis cannot easily accommodate. The marginalia in the hand that appears throughout gathering A–D are, beyond any reasonable doubt, authentic to the period. I have compared them at length with three holograph specimens in the Ashmolean, and the formation of the Greek minuscules, particularly the characteristic sigma and the peculiar elongation of the descenders in phi and psi, correspond precisely to the hand of one Giacomo Contarini, patrician of Venice, known to have numbered among Aldus's inner circle. If Contarini wrote in this book, it is exceedingly difficult to account for his presence in a forgery. Men of his station did not annotate fraudulent editions, even excellent ones.

I propose, therefore, that we arrange a meeting at your earliest convenience, at which time I shall bring both the specimens from the Ashmolean and a typed transcript of Kuchenbecker's relevant chapter, which my research assistant Miss Penrose has most obligingly rendered into English for our purposes. The matter is urgent: I understand from Dr. Munn that a certain party in Frankfurt has expressed interest in the same volume, and that the original vendor — I shall not name him in writing — is not above conducting parallel negotiations. The field, my dear Blackthorn, does not reward the contemplative disposition in matters of acquisition.

I remain, with the highest esteem and the sincerest apprehension for our shared endeavour,

Edmund Ashworth, F.B.A.
Fellow & Librarian, Magdalen College, Oxford

P.S. — I enclose a tracing of the watermark, which my wife executed with admirable precision by candlelight last Thursday evening. The original is too fragile for postal transit, but you will find the tracing sufficient for comparison with the Kuchenbecker plates.

Reply to Prof. Ashworth
